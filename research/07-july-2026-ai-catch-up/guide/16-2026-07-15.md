## Thinking Machines ships its first model as open weights

Thinking Machines released Inkling, a multimodal mixture-of-experts reasoning
model with 975 billion total parameters, 41 billion active parameters, a
one-million-token context window, and training on 45 trillion multimodal tokens.
The company explicitly said Inkling is not the strongest model overall. Its bet
is efficient inference, customization, and fine-tuning through Tinker rather
than a small benchmark lead. The release is consequential because it is a new
lab's first inspectable, deployable model artifact, not merely a preview. HN
participants compared local llama.cpp and Unsloth paths with Kimi, Qwen, Cohere,
and Nemotron, and argued that procurement and deployment constraints can matter
more than marginal benchmark position.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** Teams evaluating open models gain another large, tunable
option, but deployment cost and toolchain support deserve more weight than the
vendor's performance framing.

</div>

<div class="caveat" markdown="1">

**Caveat:** Release specifications are inspectable, but no independent
performance reproduction was found in this lane.

### OpenAI reports large gains from automated safety red-teaming

OpenAI introduced GPT-Red, an internal self-play system for finding prompt
injection and robustness failures. It reported that GPT-5.6 Sol produced six
times fewer failures than its best production model four months earlier, and
that GPT-Red attacks succeeded in 84% of replicated indirect-injection scenarios
versus 13% for humans. Those figures describe OpenAI's own evaluation, not an
independent benchmark.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** Automated adversarial generation can expand evaluation
pressure, but security decisions should not treat vendor-reported success rates
as externally validated protection.

</div>

<div class="caveat" markdown="1">

**Caveat:** All efficacy measurements are vendor-generated, and independent
reproduction was not found.

</div>

### xAI publishes Grok Build's coding-agent repository

xAI made Grok Build's repository public. The inspectable terminal agent edits
files, runs shell commands, browses the web, supports long tasks and headless CI,
and integrates through ACP. HN readers inspected implementation details such as
its terminal Mermaid renderer and browser playground, and reported Unicode and
CJK fallback problems. Exact dating remains messy: HN is timestamped July 15,
while GitHub labels the publication commit July 16.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** The repository gives teams code-level evidence for assessing
agent architecture and integration, including concrete terminal-rendering edge
cases.

</div>

<div class="caveat" markdown="1">

**Caveat:** The one-day publication conflict and limited production evidence
constrain claims about release timing and maturity.

### GPT-5.6-assisted proof claim needs mathematical review

A UC Berkeley teaching professor reported that a 148-minute GPT-5.6 Sol Pro run
produced the main argument for a near-quadratic lower bound in derivative-free
convex optimization, addressing a dimension-dependence gap dating to 1996. The
author said he checked the result and formalized it in Lean, while explicitly
stating that the linked preprint was not peer reviewed.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** Formal artifacts can make agent-assisted research easier to
audit, but theorem claims still require independent domain review before use.

</div>

<div class="caveat" markdown="1">

**Caveat:** No independent theorem audit or peer review was found; the result
must remain a claim.
