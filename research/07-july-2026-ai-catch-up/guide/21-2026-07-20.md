## Cursor quantifies swarm coordination and model economics

Cursor reported old-versus-new agent swarms building SQLite in Rust from an
835-page manual without source, test-suite knowledge, a binary, or internet.
Every new configuration eventually reached 100%; after four hours, new runs
were at 73-85% versus 11-77% for old runs. The new harness reduced conflicts,
duplicate packages, and code volume, while model mixes delivered similar final
quality at reported costs from $1,339 to $10,565. The result is less "more
agents win" than "coordination and role assignment matter." Cursor designed the
harness and evaluation, however, and HN participants challenged its
1,000-commits-per-second framing, custom VCS, and unresolved human-evaluation,
memory, context, and supervision costs.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** Benchmark planner-worker assignments, conflict rates,
completed-task cost, and human review burden before scaling agent count or
choosing one model for every role.

**Caveat:** No independent reproduction was found for this vendor-designed
experiment and held-out grader.

### AI-assisted WordPress RCE is credible; the $500,000 comparison is not

Searchlight Cyber documented a GPT-5.6-assisted path from pre-authentication
batch-API validation desynchronization through read-only SQL injection, cache
manipulation, temporary administrator identity, and a second request pass that
creates an administrator, enabling plugin upload and code execution. The author
reported just over ten model-hours and roughly $25 in prorated subscription
cost. HN security practitioners strongly disputed the headline's $500,000
exploit-broker comparison.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** AI can compress vulnerability-research iteration, but teams
still need disclosure evidence, human validation, and separate accounting for
model time, researcher time, and exploit value.

</div>

<div class="caveat" markdown="1">

**Caveat:** The technical narrative lacks an independently extracted advisory;
the $500,000 comparator is unsupported and must not be repeated as fact.

### Chinese open weights become a distribution strategy

Ben Werdmuller argues that GPU export constraints and limits on centralized
Chinese services encourage portable weight distribution, while US labs optimize
for closed services. The essay connects narrowing model gaps with ecosystem
adoption, but its conclusion that the US strategy is "doomed" is attributed
analysis rather than a measured result. HN discussion contested sustainability,
hardware incentives, state support, censorship, and open-source terminology.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** Model strategy should compare portability, hosting control,
hardware access, and ecosystem distribution, while distinguishing open weights
from open source.

</div>

### Competing agent implementations help discover the specification

Josh Bleecher Snyder describes running concurrent agent loops to build competing
distributed DNS implementations, comparing their hidden design choices, and
turning divergences into a terse specification. After about a week of his
attention, the retained system had tests, shadow deployment, documentation, and
no DNS incidents one month later. This is a bounded first-person workflow, not
comparative proof that implementation review is obsolete.

<div class="impact" markdown="1">

**Builder impact:** Use implementation diversity to surface underspecified
decisions, then preserve human-owned invariants, adversarial tests, and rollout
evidence outside the generated code.

</div>

<div class="discussion" markdown="1">

**Discussion:** HN participants stressed that specifications co-evolve with
code and plausible programs still require human validation.
